Since the creation of Bitcoin by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2009, there's been hundreds of copies based on the same basic principles but with different implementations and features.

In such a crowded space, it can be difficult to be heard. Everyone wants their altcoin (which bitcoin derivatives are called) to become widely used.

Bitcoin is by a huge margin the biggest crypto currency with the most number of users. By giving Bitcoin owners free coins of a new altcoin, the new coin instantly gets a huge distribution and acquires interest from bitcoin owners who suddenly wants to learn more about this new altcoin they own. It's a win-win.

New altcoins are distributed to bitcoin owners in two ways:
  • Forks of the Bitcoin blockchain: The new altcoin uses the same blockchain up until a certain time. Anyone who owned bitcoin at the time of the fork now owns the new altcoin as well. For example, Bitcoin Cash forked off the Bitcoin chain on August 1st, 2017 on block 478588. Anyone who owned bitcoin at that time owns the same amount of Bitcoin Cash.
  • Airdrops: Altcoins that do not fork the blockchain can still give out coins by letting Bitcoin owners prove their ownership. Normally by signing a message with your bitcoin address. An example is Byteball.

Important: You must have access to your own private Bitcoin key to claim altcoins via forks or airdrops. When you store your Bitcoins on exchanges or custodial online wallets they have your private key, not you. To have full control of your coins, use software like Bitcoin Core.

Important: A lot of these altcoins have questionable security and the software or claiming methods they use might be faulty either by poor security practises or outright ill-will with intentions to steal your bitcoin. Move your bitcoin to a new private key before claiming any altcoins. By doing so you can be sure your bitcoin are safe.
